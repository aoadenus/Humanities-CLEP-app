Greek Orders · Romanesque & Gothic · Modern Styles
I. Greek Orders
Doric Order
Earliest · c. 7th century BCE
  • Simple, unadorned flat capital (abacus + echinus)
  • No base — column sits directly on the stylobate
  • Sturdy, masculine proportions (6–7× height/diameter)
  • Shallow, parallel flutes (20 channels)
  • Entablature has alternating triglyphs and metopes
Signature Examples
Parthenon (Athens); Temple of Hephaestus; Paestum temples (Italy)
Ionic Order
Middle Order · c. 6th century BCE
  • Distinctive scroll volutes on the capital
  • Has a base (torus + scotias + torus)
  • Slender, elegant columns (8–9× height/diameter)
  • Deep channels between flutes (24 total)
  • Entablature has continuous frieze band
Signature Examples
Erechtheion (Athens); Temple of Artemis at Ephesus; Stoa of Attalos
Corinthian Order
Most Ornate · c. 5th century BCE
  • Elaborate capital with acanthus leaves and tendrils
  • Slenderest proportions (10× height/diameter)
  • Similar base to Ionic; 24 flutes
  • Associated with wealth, luxury, feminine grace
  • Favorite of the Romans — used extensively in imperial architecture
Signature Examples
Pantheon (Rome); Temple of Olympian Zeus (Athens); Roman Forum columns
II. Romanesque vs. Gothic
Romanesque
c. 1000–1200 CE · Pre-Gothic Medieval
  • Round arches — the defining feature
  • Thick, heavy walls (limited window size)
  • Low, dark interiors; sturdy piers
  • Small windows; barrel-vaulted ceilings
  • Twin towers common on façades
  • Massive, fortress-like feeling
Key Examples
Pisa Cathedral (Italy); Durham Cathedral (England); Speyer Cathedral (Germany); Santiago de Compostela (Spain)
Gothic
c. 1140–1500 CE · High Medieval
  • Pointed arches — channels thrust upward, not outward
  • Flying buttresses — transfer weight outside, free the walls
  • Ribbed vaulting — skeletal stone framework
  • Huge stained-glass windows flood interior with colored light
  • Rose windows and vertical emphasis — reaching toward God
  • Soaring, luminous, awe-inspiring interiors
Key Examples
Notre-Dame de Paris; Chartres Cathedral; Cologne Cathedral; Sainte-Chapelle (Paris); Canterbury Cathedral
III. Modern Architecture
Bauhaus
c. 1919–1933 · Dessau, Germany
  • "Form follows function" — no decoration for its own sake
  • Primary colors + black/white/grey palette
  • Industrial materials: steel, glass, concrete
  • Grid-based, asymmetric compositions
  • Unified art + craft + technology
  • Founded by Walter Gropius; closed by Nazis in 1933
Key Examples & Figures
Bauhaus Dessau Building (Gropius); Ludwig Mies van der Rohe; Marcel Breuer; László Moholy-Nagy
International Style
c. 1920s–1970s · Global
  • Curtain wall — thin glass skin on steel/concrete skeleton
  • Pure geometric volumes, no ornamentation ("ornament is crime" — Loos)
  • Flat roofs; open floor plans
  • Pilotis (stilts) to lift building off ground
  • Horizontal ribbon windows; universal grid
  • Spread globally via corporate skyscrapers
Key Examples & Figures
Seagram Building, NYC (Mies); Villa Savoye (Le Corbusier); Lever House, NYC; United Nations Secretariat
